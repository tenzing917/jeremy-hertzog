Three Things I'd Tackle

Making change communication that people actually read and trust.

Most change comms tell people what is changing without explaining why it matters to them or how it affects their actual work on a Tuesday morning. At Affinity, I built communication frameworks specifically designed to bridge that gap: what's changing, why, what it means for you, what to expect, and where to ask questions. I built messaging hierarchies, timelines, and leader toolkits so the message was consistent whether it came from a VP or a team lead.

Being a useful partner to HR and project leads, not a bottleneck.

This role isn't about leading change management. It's about making sure communication is woven into every change activity so nothing launches without people understanding it first. That's how I've always worked. At Affinity and Autodesk, I partnered across teams with very different priorities and timelines, and I earned trust by being reliable, responsive, and by delivering work that made other people's jobs easier rather than harder.

Creating real feedback channels, not suggestion boxes.

The job description mentions two-way communication and capturing employee sentiment, which I think is one of the most important and most neglected parts of change work. At Affinity, I built structured feedback loops through customer advisory boards, win/loss interviews, and research programs. The same principles apply internally. You have to create a real way for people to tell you what they're thinking, and then you have to visibly act on what you hear. I also just have a natural disposition for this. People tend to share things with me because I listen without judgment and I follow up.

Questions I'd Ask in My First Week
  • What are the highest-priority change initiatives in Global Food right now?
  • What has past change communication looked like here, what worked and what fell flat?
  • How do employees currently share feedback during transitions, and does anyone act on it?
How I Think About This Work

When an organization is changing, everyone's first question is "what does this mean for me?" The best change communication acknowledges that directly, provides real clarity, and gives people a voice in the process. Over-communicate during transitions because silence breeds anxiety. Equip leaders with talking points and toolkits so the message is consistent. And keep reinforcing after go-live, because adoption is a process that takes time, not an announcement that happens once.

How I'd Want to Be Held Accountable
  • Whether employees understand and adopt key changes.
  • Leader satisfaction with communication materials and support.
  • Timeliness and quality of delivery.
  • Fewer surprises and fewer "nobody told me" moments.
