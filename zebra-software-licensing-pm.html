Software Licensing Product Manager

I've spent my career helping companies make sense of complicated things. Software licensing is where product strategy, pricing, customer value, and sales enablement all intersect, and that intersection is exactly where I do my best work.

Why I Want This Job

Zebra sits at the center of something I find genuinely interesting: the shift from hardware-defined to software-defined value where the stakes are real and measurable. Shrink, inventory accuracy, customer satisfaction. Software licensing is how Zebra deepens those customer relationships from a one-time hardware purchase to an ongoing partnership. I've spent the last several years doing exactly this: building recurring revenue models, defining pricing strategies, and translating complex product value into language sales teams and customers actually understand. The fact that it's a company with real hardware-software complexity and an embedded customer base, not just another pure SaaS play, makes it more interesting to me, not less.

Training and enabling the people who actually sell and support it.

A product launch isn't done when the press release goes out. It's done when the sales rep can walk a customer through the value in their own words. At Affinity, I built enablement from scratch: battlecards, competitive briefs, onboarding materials, advisory board presentations. Questions I'd Ask in My First Week
  • What does the current licensing model look like across on-prem and remote deployments, and where are the biggest friction points for customers and for the sales team?
  • How are license usage data and entitlement tracking handled today, and how reliable is the reporting pipeline? Where are the known discrepancies?
  • How are customers currently evaluating embedded SDK alternatives versus Zebra's software offerings, and what's driving those decisions?
  • What does the voice-of-customer feedback loop look like right now? How is customer input getting into product and pricing decisions?
  • What's the relationship between the hardware sales motion and the software licensing motion today, and where does it break down?
  • Which features do customers say matter most, and where does that diverge from what we're prioritizing on the roadmap?
How I Think About This Work

Software licensing product management is about making the value exchange clear: what does the customer get, what does it cost, and why is that a good deal for both sides? Everything else, the financial modeling, competitive analysis, pricing tiers, training, launch plans, serves that central question. What separates good product managers from great ones is how seriously they take the voice of the customer. Not just collecting feedback, but building a system where customer input directly shapes feature prioritization, pricing, and the roadmap. I've built those systems, and I've worked in scrum environments validating that development tracks to spec and market need. The best product managers move fluently between a spreadsheet, a customer conversation, and a sprint review, because all three are telling you the same thing from different angles.
