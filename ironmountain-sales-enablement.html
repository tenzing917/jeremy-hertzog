Three Things I'd Tackle

Designing and deploying enablement programs that change how sellers actually sell.

At Affinity, I helped build the sales enablement function alongside our Head of Competitive Intelligence as the company scaled from $17M to $60M in ARR. She supplied the competitive data and win/loss analysis; I took that, combined it with my market expertise and customer insights, and turned it into the actual enablement content: battlecards, onboarding curricula, case studies, talk tracks, demo frameworks, and training. I owned the output and delivered the training to sales. At Autodesk, I earned my Command of the Message certification through Force Management, which gave me a structured framework for value-based selling that I've applied at every company since. The enablement I build isn't a library of PDFs that nobody reads. It's integrated into the selling motion: tied to deal stages, reinforced in coaching sessions, and measured by whether it actually moves pipeline metrics.

Using data to find performance gaps and prove enablement impact.

At Affinity, I partnered with our Head of Competitive Intelligence to build the company's first structured win/loss analysis program. I designed the questionnaire to accurately capture voice of customer, industry context, and product-level insights, and I kept it refreshed as the market evolved. From there, I was the person who turned those insights into action: translating win/loss findings into product roadmap narratives for leadership, sales enablement materials, competitive positioning on the website and at events, messaging frameworks, demand gen campaign strategy, customer marketing, and content. I tracked conversion rates, deal velocity, and pipeline coverage to connect competitive pressure to performance gaps. I presented these findings to the executive team quarterly, including recommendations that sometimes meant killing initiatives the team was excited about because the data didn't support them. Enablement that can't prove its own impact isn't enablement; it's content production.

Partnering with senior sales leaders to drive forecasting discipline and deal qualification.

At Affinity, I worked directly with the VP of Sales and segment leaders on competitive deals, bringing market intelligence and customer research into the deal strategy. At Autodesk, I sat in deal reviews for TradeTapp and brought the outside-in perspective: what the customer was actually evaluating, what the competitive alternatives looked like, and where the value narrative needed to shift. I've also built the frameworks that make these conversations productive, structured qualification criteria, stage-gate definitions, and the coaching questions that help sellers self-diagnose where a deal is actually stuck versus where they think it is.

Questions I'd Ask in My First Week
  • What commercial methodologies is the team currently using, and how consistently are they being applied across sellers?
  • Where are the biggest pipeline health issues right now: top-of-funnel coverage, mid-funnel velocity, or late-stage conversion?
  • How is enablement success currently measured, and what metrics does leadership care about most?
  • What does the onboarding experience look like today for new commercial team members?
  • How does the Sales Enablement Manager work with Sales Operations day-to-day on forecasting and pipeline management?
How I Think About This Work
The best enablement programs don't just teach sellers what to say. They build the muscle memory for how to think about a deal: how to qualify honestly, how to advance with discipline, and how to forecast with confidence. I've always believed that enablement is a feedback loop. You build the framework, you train the team, you measure what happens, and then you use what you learn to make the framework better. That loop is what turns enablement from a support function into a revenue driver.
How I'd Want to Be Held Accountable
  • Pipeline velocity and conversion rate improvements tied to specific enablement initiatives
  • Forecasting accuracy tracked quarter-over-quarter with clear accountability for adherence to methodology
  • Onboarding time-to-productivity measured against benchmarks
  • Enablement adoption metrics: not just completion rates, but actual usage in live selling situations
  • Quarterly business impact reviews connecting enablement activity to revenue outcomes
