How I Can Help St. Luke's

I've spent my career helping companies make sense of complicated things: product stories, brand narratives, internal communications, and go-to-market strategy. I'm moving home to Boise and I want to do that work here.

Why St. Luke's

St. Luke's is Idaho's largest employer and its only locally owned, not-for-profit health system. That combination matters. It means the brand story isn't just marketing — it's a commitment to a community that I'm choosing to come back to and raise my family in.

St. Luke's is also in the middle of something significant. The Boise campus expansion is a multi-year project adding patient beds, operating rooms, and advanced diagnostic capabilities, all while the hospitals stay fully operational. You're growing physical infrastructure, evolving how you deliver care, and managing the communication challenges that come with a system spanning eight hospitals and thousands of providers. That kind of growth creates real communication and brand challenges, and those are the exact problems I've spent my career solving.

The Problems I Solve

Keeping a brand story coherent across a large, multi-site system.

St. Luke's operates eight hospitals, dozens of clinics, a health plan, and a clinically integrated network serving communities across central and southern Idaho. Each site has its own culture and its own patient population. When a system operates at that scale, the risk is that the brand starts meaning something different in every location. At Affinity, I built the messaging architecture that became the company-wide source of truth for how the company described itself, its products, and its value to customers. That framework held as the company grew from $17M to $60M ARR and went through multiple reorgs and leadership changes. At Right This Way Flooring, I built an entire brand from scratch: name, voice, sales language, referral program, in-store experience. I've built brand systems from zero and I've maintained them through rapid growth. Both are hard in different ways.

Communicating through growth, expansion, and organizational change.

The Boise campus expansion will run through 2030. That's years of construction, new capabilities, new workflows, and new hires, all while the existing system continues serving patients. On top of that, St. Luke's is navigating the broader shifts happening across healthcare. At Affinity, I led communication strategy through multiple reorgs, leadership transitions, and shifting company strategy during scaling from $17M to $60M ARR. At Jelli, I was employee #25 when we were acquired by iHeartMedia and helped shape how the combined organization communicated through a completely new identity and structure. I know what good change communication looks like during those moments, and what bad communication costs in engagement, trust, and retention.

Making complex services feel clear and human to different audiences.

Healthcare communication is inherently multi-audience. Patients, providers, employees, community partners, donors, and policymakers all need to understand what St. Luke's does and why it matters, but they each need it framed differently. At Autodesk, I owned a construction risk product called TradeTapp and built persona-specific content for general contractors, subcontractors, and risk managers, each with different workflows, language, and technical comfort levels. At Affinity, I wrote for audiences as different as junior analysts and Fortune 500 managing partners and made both groups feel like the message was for them. The trick is always specificity. Real stories about real people doing real work outperform corporate generalities every time.

Building the systems that make marketing and communications actually work.

I don't just create content. I build the processes and infrastructure that make content production repeatable, measurable, and useful to the people who need it. At Affinity, I built competitive intelligence programs, win/loss analysis, customer advisory boards, battlecards, and sales enablement toolkits. I managed agencies and offshore talent, coordinated product launches with tight cross-functional dependencies, and produced executive materials requiring alignment through senior leadership. When the system works, the work gets better and faster over time instead of starting from scratch every quarter.

How I Think About the Work

The best healthcare communication doesn't come from a marketing team writing about the organization from the outside. It comes from going into the system, finding the clinicians and teams doing remarkable work, and helping them tell their own story in a way that reflects what the organization stands for. The core narrative stays the same, but how you tell it has to shift by audience, channel, and moment. And the acid test is always the same: if the people closest to the work won't use it, it's not done yet.

Working With Me
  • I ask a lot of questions in the first few weeks. Not because I'm unsure of myself, but because I've learned that listening before acting leads to much better work.
  • I write things down. Frameworks, processes, playbooks. If I build something useful, I document it so the next person can use it too.
  • I'm direct with feedback and I welcome it in return. I'd rather hear something is off early than find out after it ships.
  • I've managed people, agencies, and cross-functional projects. I'm comfortable leading and comfortable being a senior individual contributor. Both are real work.
  • I care about doing good work more than getting credit for it.
