How I Can Help Saint Alphonsus

I've spent my career helping companies make sense of complicated things: product stories, brand narratives, internal communications, and go-to-market strategy. I'm moving home to Boise and I want to do that work here.

Why Saint Alphonsus

I grew up in Boise, and Saint Alphonsus has been part of this community for over 125 years. That kind of continuity means something, especially as I move my family back to a place where institutions with deep roots matter.

Saint Alphonsus also sits in an interesting position. You're a faith-based, community-rooted health system serving Idaho, eastern Oregon, and northern Nevada, and you're part of Trinity Health, one of the largest Catholic health systems in the country. That means navigating a dual identity: the local mission and the national organization, the community hospital and the broader system. I've lived through exactly that kind of organizational complexity. When Jelli was acquired by iHeartMedia, I helped shape how a small company with a strong identity communicated through integration into a much larger corporate parent, preserving what worked while adapting to new realities. That experience taught me how to balance local voice with organizational alignment, and I'd bring that directly to Saint Alphonsus.

The Problems I Solve

Telling a mission-driven story that doesn't sound like every other health system.

Saint Alphonsus's identity is distinct: faith-based, not-for-profit, rooted in the values of compassion, reverence, and service. But the challenge with mission-driven organizations is that the language can start to feel generic if it's not constantly grounded in real stories about real people doing real work. At Affinity, I built the messaging architecture that connected the corporate narrative to individual product lines to sales materials to customer case studies, adopted from the boardroom to the field across the company. At Right This Way Flooring, I built an entire brand identity from scratch in a commoditized market where nobody expected differentiation. The principle is the same: find what's genuinely true about the organization and make it specific enough that people feel it.

Balancing a local identity with a national parent organization.

Being part of Trinity Health gives Saint Alphonsus resources, scale, and capabilities that independent systems can't match. But it also creates tension: how do you maintain the feel of a local institution while operating within a 22-state system? At Jelli, I navigated exactly this when iHeartMedia acquired us. I shaped both internal and external messaging through the acquisition, helping a combined organization figure out who it was now, what to keep, what to adapt, and how to communicate through the transition without losing the people who made the original company work. At Affinity, I led communication strategy through multiple reorgs and leadership transitions, building the messaging frameworks that kept teams aligned when everything was changing around them.

Communicating complex healthcare information to diverse audiences.

Saint Alphonsus serves patients, families, providers, employees, community partners, and donors across three states. Each audience needs a different version of the same truth. At Autodesk, I owned TradeTapp, a construction risk product, and built persona-specific content for general contractors, subcontractors, and risk managers who all had different workflows, language, and technical comfort levels. At Affinity, I wrote for audiences ranging from junior analysts to Fortune 500 managing partners and made both groups feel like the message was for them. I know how to take a complex story and make it land differently for people who think about it differently.

Building communication infrastructure, not just content.

At Affinity, I built competitive intelligence programs, win/loss analysis, customer advisory boards, and the full enablement toolkit: battlecards, talk tracks, case studies, and presentation decks. I managed agencies and offshore talent, coordinated cross-functional launches, and produced executive materials requiring alignment through senior leadership. I don't just write things. I build the systems, processes, and frameworks that make communications work consistently over time, so the quality doesn't depend on any one person being in the room.

How I Think About the Work

The best communication in a mission-driven organization comes from the people closest to the mission. My job is to find those stories, the physician who stayed late, the team that innovated under pressure, the community program that changed someone's life, and help the organization tell them in a way that's true, specific, and unmistakably Saint Alphonsus. Not generic healthcare language. Real stories from real people doing work that reflects the values this organization was built on.
