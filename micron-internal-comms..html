Three Things I'd Tackle

Advising leaders on what to say and how to say it, for every kind of audience.

At Affinity, I worked directly with the CEO, CMO, CPO, and Chief People Officer to shape messaging for some of the hardest communication moments a company faces: entering new markets, shifting from SMB to enterprise, launching new products, changing pricing and packaging, announcing reorgs, onboarding new executives, and saying goodbye to departing leaders. I didn't just write the message. I partnered with leadership to align on the core narrative, then owned turning that into differentiated materials for every audience layer: executive talking points, manager-to-team guidance, org-wide announcements, town hall scripts, and QBR presentations. That's the muscle this role needs.

Building the communication infrastructure, not just sending messages.

I think of internal comms as a system that needs to be designed, maintained, and improved over time. At Affinity, I built messaging frameworks, communications plans, wikis that teams could reference on their own, and toolkits tailored for different layers of leadership. I ran dry-runs with speakers before town halls and company-wide events, created the scripting and production flow, and built cadences that gave people a predictable rhythm of information during unpredictable stretches. I'd bring that same approach to TPG: an annual communications strategy anchored to the organization's priorities, with a clear framework for what gets built against each one.

Translating technical complexity for a global, distributed workforce.

I've spent my career in technical environments where the subject matter is genuinely hard to explain. At Autodesk, I owned TradeTapp, a construction risk analysis product, and had to speak the language of general contractors, subcontractors, and risk managers, all differently. At Jelli, I learned how programmatic radio advertising worked from the hardware installations in broadcast stations to the two-sided marketplace where media buyers scheduled campaigns. At Affinity, I translated a CRM built on complex data enrichment, API integrations, and automated workflows into messaging that worked for analysts and managing partners alike. TPG is a technical organization doing deeply complex work. I'm comfortable being the person who sits between the experts and everyone else and makes the translation land across sites and regions.

How I Think About This Work

Every message from leadership is either building trust or spending it. The best internal communicators aren't just good writers. They understand the political landscape, the emotional undercurrent, and the gap between what a leader means and what an employee hears. I've handled confidential communications around stealth product launches, market entries that hadn't been announced yet, and restructures that required careful sequencing. I know what it means for comms to be on-call, and I know the judgment required when the stakes are high and the timeline is short. I start every piece of communication by asking: after someone reads this, what should they understand, believe, and do?

How I'd Want to Be Held Accountable
  • Whether people actually understood the message during transitions, not just whether they opened it.
  • Whether leaders feel well-supported with materials, talking points, and strategy before they walk into a room.
  • Whether "I didn't know about that" moments get rarer across sites and regions.
  • Whether the communications framework keeps pace with the organization's priorities as they evolve.
