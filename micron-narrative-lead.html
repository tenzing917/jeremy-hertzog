Three Things I'd Tackle

Building a message house that actually gets used from the boardroom to the field.

At Affinity, I built the messaging architecture that connected the corporate story to individual product lines to sales pitch materials to customer case studies. Then I built the toolkits that made it actionable: battlecards, elevator pitches, talk tracks, competitive comparisons, presentation decks. Those materials directly shortened sales cycles and improved win rates. A message house is only as good as its adoption, and adoption comes from building something people find genuinely useful in their day-to-day work.

Closing the gap between intended message and what the field actually says.

The biggest narrative problem in large organizations is that headquarters writes one thing and the field says something else. I've addressed this by building messaging with the field, not just for them. At Affinity, my win/loss program, customer interviews, and sales conversations gave me direct insight into what language actually landed with buyers and what fell flat. That intelligence shaped the narrative rather than the other way around.

Keeping the story current in a market that moves fast.

The AI and semiconductor landscape shifts constantly, so a narrative framework built once and updated annually is already stale by the time it ships. At Affinity, I ran continuous competitive intelligence, customer advisory boards, and research loops that fed fresh data into messaging on an ongoing basis. I build living narrative systems, not one-time documents.

Questions I'd Ask in My First Week
  • What does the current master narrative look like, and when was it last meaningfully updated?
  • Where are the biggest inconsistencies in how Micron's story gets told across business units and regions?
  • What do sales reps actually say when they pitch Micron, and how far is that from the intended message?
How I Think About This Work

A corporate narrative answers four questions: Who are we? What do we believe? What value do we create? Why does it matter right now? The best narratives are built on customer truth and real differentiation, not aspirational language. And the acid test is always the same: if the field won't use it, it's not done.
